For most Nepalis with savings, the investment universe is small: fixed deposits, real estate, gold, and NEPSE. That's it. Access to global markets is legally restricted, most mutual funds are opaque, and financial literacy infrastructure barely exists outside Kathmandu.

That's about to change — and the window to build trust with that audience is open right now.

## The Setup

Nepal has roughly 65% of its population under 35. Smartphone penetration crossed 50% in 2023 and is climbing. The remittance economy — over $9 billion annually, nearly 25% of GDP — means there's a real base of people with money coming in who don't have sophisticated places to put it.

> "The country is pre-financial. Not unfinancial. Pre-financial."

That distinction matters enormously for what comes next.

## What's Changing

Three things are converging:

**Regulatory signals.** Nepal Rastra Bank has been cautiously studying frameworks for allowing retail participation in foreign equity markets. It won't happen tomorrow. But the conversation is happening, which it wasn't five years ago.

**Mobile payment infrastructure.** eSewa, Khalti, and others have normalized the idea of holding and moving money digitally. The behavioral shift from cash to digital happened faster in Nepal than most predicted.

**Peer learning.** A generation of Nepalis who've lived or studied abroad have seen how retail investing works in India, the US, and the Gulf. They come back with expectations. Those expectations create demand pressure on regulators.

## The Opportunity for Media and Education

Before a single rupee flows into a new investment product, someone has to explain it. That's the role financial media plays in the early stages of market development — not just news, but translation.

The people who build that credibility now, when the audience is small, are the ones who own it when the audience scales.
