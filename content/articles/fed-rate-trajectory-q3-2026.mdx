The consensus view on Fed rates heading into Q3 2026 goes something like this: inflation is trending down, the labor market is softening, and the Fed will cut 2-3 times before year-end. Markets have priced this in. Bonds have rallied on the expectation. Tech multiples have expanded accordingly.

I think this is probably right. But there's a scenario the consensus is underweighting, and it's worth thinking through carefully.

## Fiscal Dominance — The Quiet Variable

The US government is running a deficit that, at current interest rates, requires significant debt rollover at rates far higher than the bonds being replaced. The interest expense line on the federal budget is now the fastest-growing category, having surpassed defense spending.

This creates a subtle but powerful pressure: the longer rates stay high, the more the fiscal situation deteriorates, which eventually creates political pressure on monetary policy that independent central banks are supposed to resist — but historically, don't always.

**Fiscal dominance** is the condition where monetary policy becomes subordinated to the government's funding needs. The Fed is not there. But the trajectory matters.

## The Scenario: Stagflation Lite

Here's what I'm watching for:

1. Inflation re-accelerates modestly in Q2 (driven by services and shelter, not goods)
2. Labor market stays resilient — unemployment stays below 4.5%
3. The Fed holds in June, signals caution
4. Bond markets price out 2026 cuts entirely
5. Dollar strengthens; EM currencies including NPR/INR feel pressure
6. Fed cuts anyway in September — not because inflation is beaten, but because something in credit markets breaks

That last step is the non-consensus part. The Fed tends to cut reactively, not proactively. If they cut into still-elevated inflation because of credit stress, that's the stagflation-lite scenario — and it's bad for most portfolios.

## What I'd Hold Through This

- Short-duration bonds over long-duration
- Real assets: commodity exposure, infrastructure
- Non-dollar denominated savings (gold in NPR terms has been quietly exceptional)
- Cash optionality — dry powder for when the credit break creates buying opportunities

## My Prediction (On Record)

The Fed cuts once in 2026 — in Q4, not Q3 — and the cut is reactive, not proactive. 10-year yields end the year between 4.25% and 4.75%. Dollar remains strong through Q3, weakens in Q4.

I'll revisit this in September.
