The dominant narrative in Western financial media frames US-China tensions as a binary: decoupling, deglobalization, the end of the supply chain era we knew. That framing is wrong — or at least incomplete.

What's actually happening is more interesting, and for investors paying attention from outside the Western bubble, potentially more profitable.

## Capital Doesn't Disappear. It Reroutes.

When the US started imposing tariffs on Chinese goods in 2018, economists predicted a significant reduction in global trade volumes. What happened instead was a surge in trade through third countries — Vietnam, Mexico, India, Indonesia. Supply chains didn't shorten; they *bent*.

The same principle applies to capital flows today. Institutional money that once went directly into China-facing positions is now flowing into:

- Southeast Asian manufacturing hubs (Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia)
- Indian infrastructure and consumer plays
- Gulf sovereign wealth vehicles as geopolitical neutral ground
- Commodity exporters that both China and the West need

## Why This Matters From Kathmandu

Nepal sits between two of the world's largest economies — India and China — and is directly affected by capital flows into both. As India becomes the preferred "China plus one" destination for global manufacturers, Indian capital markets deepen and stabilize. That has direct implications for NPR/INR dynamics and for Nepali businesses with cross-border exposure.

More importantly: the infrastructure buildout accompanying this rerouting — ports, roads, logistics corridors — creates real economic activity that eventually lifts frontier economies in the region.

## The Trade I'm Watching

The asymmetric bet here is on Indian infrastructure-adjacent plays. As manufacturing relocates to India, power demand, logistics capacity, and digital infrastructure all need to scale. The companies building that backbone — not the consumer tech darlings — are the ones I'd be looking at over a 3-5 year horizon.

This isn't a short-term trade. It's a structural thesis. And the best time to build conviction in a structural thesis is before it becomes consensus.
